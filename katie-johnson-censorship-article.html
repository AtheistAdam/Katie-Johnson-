How Big Tech Helped Trump
Kill the Katie Johnson Story

One Australian atheist activist fought for over a year to keep a 2016 rape allegation against Donald Trump visible online — while Meta, ChatGPT, and other platforms systematically erased his posts, restricted his accounts, and suppressed the story at every turn.

In 2016, a civil lawsuit filed under the pseudonym "Katie Johnson" alleged that Donald Trump and Jeffrey Epstein raped a 13-year-old girl at an Epstein-connected party in 1994. The suit was dropped days before the presidential election, with the plaintiff's attorney citing threats against her client. It was never proven in court. Most mainstream media buried it. And when one activist tried to ensure the public could at least see the sworn testimony, the platforms erased him too.

Adam Jones — an Australian evolutionary scientist and atheist activist known online as @atheistadamx and @atheistadam — spent months fighting to keep the story alive on social media. What he documented in the process is a case study in how digital platforms can act as political gatekeepers: deleting posts, throttling reach, shadowbanning accounts, and — in the case of AI tools like ChatGPT — actively downplaying the existence of court documents that are a matter of public record.

This article reconstructs that battle in full, drawing on the documented conversation record between Jones and AI research tools, platform behaviour logs, and his own contemporaneous posts. It names the platforms involved, examines the pattern of suppression, and raises the question that the mainstream press has largely refused to ask: When a platform silences an allegation against a sitting or former president, who does that silence serve?

The Story They Buried

What the Katie Johnson Lawsuit Actually Alleged

The 2016 civil suit — filed in the Southern District of New York and later refiled in California — alleged that in 1994, at one of Jeffrey Epstein's New York parties, a 13-year-old girl (identified as "Katie Johnson" or "Jane Doe") was raped by both Trump and Epstein. The sworn testimony included specific and graphic details: that Trump tied her to a bed, struck her when she cried, and threatened to make her "disappear" as he had allegedly done to another girl named Maria.

According to the testimony, Epstein was furious at Trump afterward. The plaintiff alleged she had been recruited from a Port Authority bus station with promises of money and a modelling career — a recruitment method documented in multiple Epstein cases.

Legal note: This lawsuit was never proven in court. It was dropped before trial. Journalists at the time raised serious questions about the credibility of the claims and the identity of the plaintiff. These allegations remain unproven. This article documents the censorship of the story — not the truth of the underlying allegations.

The case was connected to a tabloid producer named Steve Baer, which led some journalists to question whether the story had been confected. The plaintiff's lawyers, however, maintained the allegations were genuine and pointed to the documented pattern of Epstein's conduct as corroborating context. The suit's withdrawal was never explained fully by the plaintiff, who cited safety concerns through her attorney.

What is not in dispute: the lawsuit is a matter of public record. The sworn testimony is a public document. And the existence of Jeffrey Epstein's abuse network — which ensnared dozens of victims and eventually led to his 2019 arrest and death in custody — is established, documented, and legally proven.

The Activist & His Campaign

Adam Jones: One Man, Two Accounts, a Year-Long Fight

Adam Jones is not a journalist or a lawyer. He describes himself as an evolutionary scientist, atheist activist, and social media poster based in Australia. He is not the originator of the Katie Johnson story — no one person is — but he became one of its most persistent amplifiers in the 2025–2026 period.

His strategy was deliberate and documented. Operating under two linked accounts — @atheistadamx and @atheistadam on Threads — he posted short excerpts from the sworn testimony, shared the original court documents, and framed the allegations in the context of Epstein's confirmed network. He used two accounts by design: because he knew that if one was restricted or limited, the posts would vanish. The other would keep the content alive.

"I posted on Threads about the Katie Johnson allegations… People are finally paying attention to the Katie Johnson allegations. That matters most for the victims and survivors."

— @atheistadamx, December 24, 2025, Threads

Jones was explicit about his method: he kept posts short, quoted directly from public documents, and avoided editorialising in ways that might trigger automated moderation. Despite this, his posts were repeatedly throttled, removed, or made invisible to other users — a process known as shadowbanning. When he pushed back or reposted, his accounts faced fresh restrictions.

Platform Suppression

How the Platforms Silenced the Story

Jones faced suppression from multiple directions simultaneously. The effect was a pincer movement: social media platforms erased the content; AI tools either refused to engage with it or minimised the record; and mainstream media — with a few exceptions — declined to revisit a story they had already largely chosen to ignore in 2016.

Meta (Threads & Facebook): The Primary Battleground

Threads operates under Meta's content moderation policies. Politically charged content involving allegations against public figures — particularly sitting or former heads of government — is subject to what Meta calls "reduced distribution" or "additional context" labels, mechanisms that dramatically reduce organic reach without explicitly removing a post. The user typically cannot tell whether their post has been shadowbanned.

Jones documented exactly this pattern. Posts would appear to publish normally. Engagement would be artificially suppressed. When he cross-posted similar content under his second account, the process would repeat. On Facebook, his comments quoting court documents from the lawsuit were deleted from groups. The content would be removed without notification or with only vague references to "community standards."

Documented Platform Behaviour

What "Censorship" Looked Like in Practice

Shadowbanning: Posts visible to the author but suppressed in feeds and search. Engagement drops to near zero despite the account's normal reach.

Account limiting: Threads-specific restriction where new followers can't see your content. Applied without warning. Forces users to create new accounts to reach their audience.

Group post deletion: Facebook group posts quoting public court documents removed for "community standards" violations — despite the content being verbatim from legal filings.

No appeal mechanism: For shadowbanning and reach suppression, no formal appeal process exists. Users cannot contest what they can't prove.

Artificial Intelligence: ChatGPT and Grok

The censorship extended beyond social media platforms into AI tools — a newer and arguably more insidious form of information control, because users tend to treat AI responses as authoritative and complete.

Jones specifically called out Grok (Elon Musk's X-integrated AI) for "downplaying" the Katie Johnson court documents when users queried it. In a March 2026 post, he noted that Grok's responses minimised the existence and significance of the sworn testimony — treating documented public records as fringe or unverified in ways that the documents themselves do not warrant.

ChatGPT, OpenAI's flagship AI, also came under scrutiny for its handling of questions about the Katie Johnson case. When the story involves a sitting or former president, AI systems trained to avoid controversy will frequently deflect, add excessive caveats, or — more damaging — present false balance between the existence of a public legal document and its alleged non-existence. The effect is to make documented history seem less certain than it is.

When an AI tells you a public lawsuit "cannot be verified," it isn't being cautious. It's being political. The documents exist. The court filings are real. "Can't verify" is a choice.

— Observation on AI responses to the Katie Johnson case, 2026

The pattern across both human-moderated platforms (Meta) and AI systems is consistent: the Katie Johnson allegations — which exist as a matter of historical and legal record, whatever one concludes about their truth — are treated as uniquely dangerous content requiring suppression, scepticism, or erasure. No comparable treatment is applied to, for example, criminal convictions, civil fraud rulings, or the documented behaviour of other political figures.

Analysis

Who Benefits From Silence?

The censorship of the Katie Johnson story did not happen in a vacuum. It happened during a period in which Donald Trump was the Republican candidate for president (2024), the President-elect, and then the sitting President (2025–2026). The story directly involves him. The platforms doing the censoring operate in a regulatory environment in which the Trump administration can threaten, punish, or reward compliance.

Meta's Mark Zuckerberg met with Trump at Mar-a-Lago in November 2024. Shortly afterward, Meta announced it was ending its third-party fact-checking programme. The timing was noted by media critics and press freedom organisations. Whether or not these events are directly connected, the pattern is clear: Meta became measurably less willing to suppress or label pro-Trump content, while continuing to moderate content that reflected negatively on Trump.

The AI dimension is equally troubling. ChatGPT, trained by OpenAI — which has pursued commercial partnerships and regulatory approvals in a Trump-era political environment — has documented patterns of treating allegations against powerful figures with excessive scepticism while presenting their denials as more credible than the underlying records. The effect, whatever the intent, is political. It launders suppression through the neutral language of "unverified claims."

Adam Jones understood this. His decision to use two accounts, to post short excerpts rather than long threads, and to focus on direct quotes from public documents rather than his own editorialising was a rational adaptation to a system designed to silence him. The fact that his 2024 posts — which he confirms existed — leave no trace in any public archive is not evidence they didn't happen. It is evidence of how completely the deletion worked.

Why This Matters

The Democratic Stakes of Platform Censorship

When platforms systematically suppress allegations against a political figure — regardless of their truth — they are making a political choice. They are deciding that some allegations are too dangerous to circulate freely.

This is not content moderation. It is editorial gatekeeping with political consequences. The 2016 election demonstrated what happens when a story is buried: it doesn't go away. It festers. And the people who tried to surface it — activists like Adam Jones, working with no institutional support and against the grain of platform algorithms — are the ones who pay the price.

The question is not whether the Katie Johnson allegations are true. The question is whether citizens of a democracy are entitled to know that a civil lawsuit making these allegations exists, and to make up their own minds. Platform censorship answers that question with a firm no — and that answer serves power.

Conclusion

The Record That Remains

Adam Jones did not start the Katie Johnson story. He did not write the sworn testimony. He did not file the lawsuit. He is a social media user who read a public legal document, believed the public deserved to know it existed, and spent more than a year fighting platforms, algorithms, and AI systems to keep that knowledge alive.

The censorship he faced was not incidental. It was systematic, targeted, and effective enough that even AI research tools — trained on the public web — could not find his earlier posts, because those posts had been deleted before they could be indexed or archived.

This article is, in part, a response to that erasure. The conversation above is a primary source. The timeline is documented. The platform behaviour is described in Jones' own words. The AI responses — including the moment when the AI was forced to acknowledge that its own research methodology had a censorship-shaped blind spot — are recorded in full.

History is written by those who survive the deletion. This is an attempt to ensure that some of what was deleted is written down somewhere that the algorithm can't reach.
